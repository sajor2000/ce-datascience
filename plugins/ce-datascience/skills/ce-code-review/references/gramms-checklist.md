# GRAMMS Checklist for Mixed-Methods Studies

Good Reporting of A Mixed Methods Study (GRAMMS).
6-item checklist for reporting studies that combine qualitative and quantitative methods.

**Primary reference:** O'Cathain A, Murphy E, Nicholl J. The quality of mixed methods studies in health services research. J Health Serv Res Policy. 2008;13(2):92-98. doi:10.1258/jhsrp.2007.007074. PMID: 18416914.

Apply when reporting studies that deliberately combine qualitative and quantitative methods as part of a single investigation. GRAMMS focuses on whether the rationale for mixing methods is clear, whether the design is described coherently, and whether the integration of the two strands is explained and evaluated. It applies to published reports of empirical studies using mixed methods in health services research and related fields.

---

## Justification

### Item 1 -- Justification for using a mixed methods approach

**Description:** Describe why a mixed methods approach was used for this study. Explain the specific value added by combining methods that could not be achieved by using either approach alone.

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- Explicit statement of why neither qualitative nor quantitative methods alone were sufficient
- Mixed methods purpose articulated (triangulation, complementarity, development, initiation, expansion)
- Description of how each methodological strand addresses a different aspect of the research question
- Reference to a mixed methods rationale framework or typology (Greene et al., Creswell, Tashakkori and Teddlie)

---

## Design

### Item 2 -- Description of the mixed methods design

**Description:** Identify the mixed methods design used (e.g., convergent, explanatory sequential, exploratory sequential, embedded). Describe the timing of the two strands (concurrent or sequential), the relative emphasis or weight given to each strand (equal or dominant), and the point of integration.

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- Named mixed methods design type with citation supporting the design framework
- Timing specified (concurrent data collection vs. sequential with strand order described)
- Relative priority or weighting of qualitative and quantitative strands stated
- Point of integration identified (at data collection, analysis, interpretation, or all three)
- Visual diagram or notation describing the design (e.g., QUAL + quan, QUAN -> qual)

---

## Data Collection

### Item 3 -- Rationale for data collection techniques

**Description:** Describe the specific qualitative and quantitative data collection techniques used and explain why these techniques were chosen for each strand of the study.

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- Quantitative data collection method described (survey instrument, administrative data extraction, randomized trial, cohort)
- Qualitative data collection method described (interviews, focus groups, observation, documents)
- Rationale for choosing each technique linked to the research question and design
- Sampling strategy for each strand described (purposive, random, theoretical, convenience)
- Sample sizes for each strand with justification (saturation, power, representativeness)

---

## Analysis

### Item 4 -- Description of analysis methods

**Description:** Describe the methods used to analyze data in each strand. Explain any procedures used to integrate or link data from the two strands during the analysis phase.

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- Quantitative analysis method specified (regression, survival analysis, descriptive statistics, SPC)
- Qualitative analysis method specified (thematic analysis, grounded theory, framework analysis, IPA)
- Software tools for each strand named (R, Stata, SPSS for quantitative; NVivo, Atlas.ti, MAXQDA for qualitative)
- Integration analysis procedures described if applicable (joint display, case-ordered matrix, transformation)
- Inter-rater reliability or triangulation procedures for qualitative coding documented

---

## Integration

### Item 5 -- Description of how integration was achieved

**Description:** Describe how the qualitative and quantitative strands were brought together (integrated) to address the research question. Report the findings from both strands in relation to each other. Identify any divergent or contradictory findings across strands.

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- Integration narrative linking qualitative themes to quantitative results
- Joint display table or matrix presenting findings from both strands side by side
- Convergent findings identified and described
- Divergent or contradictory findings across strands explicitly reported and discussed
- Meta-inferences drawn from integration stated separately from single-strand conclusions
- Mixed methods findings presented as distinct from the sum of individual strand findings

---

## Quality

### Item 6 -- Identification of the limitations of each approach and quality criteria applied

**Description:** Discuss the quality of the qualitative and quantitative strands separately, and the quality of the integration. Address limitations of each method, including threats to internal validity, reliability, credibility, and transferability. Describe any quality criteria applied that are specific to mixed methods research.

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- Quantitative limitations addressed (selection bias, confounding, measurement error, generalizability)
- Qualitative limitations addressed (reflexivity, researcher positionality, transferability, confirmability)
- Integration quality discussed (completeness of integration, whether divergence was fully explored)
- Quality criteria specific to mixed methods applied (legitimation, inference quality, integration fidelity)
- Reflexivity statement documenting researchers' positionality relative to the study context
- Member checking or respondent validation described for qualitative strand
- Qualitative rigor strategies reported (audit trail, negative case analysis, thick description)

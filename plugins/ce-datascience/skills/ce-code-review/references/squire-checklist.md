# SQUIRE 2.0 Checklist for Quality Improvement Studies

Standards for QUality Improvement Reporting Excellence (SQUIRE 2.0).
18-item checklist for reporting quality improvement work in healthcare.

**Primary reference:** Ogrinc G, Davies L, Goodman D, Batalden P, Davidoff F, Stevens D. SQUIRE 2.0 (Standards for QUality Improvement Reporting Excellence): revised publication guidelines from a detailed consensus process. BMJ Qual Saf. 2016;25(12):986-992. doi:10.1136/bmjqs-2015-004411. PMID: 25505110.

**Explanation and elaboration:** Goodman D, Ogrinc G, Davies L, Baker GR, Barnsteiner J, Foster TC, et al. Explanation and elaboration of the SQUIRE (Standards for Quality Improvement Reporting Excellence) Guidelines, V.2.0: examples of SQUIRE elements in the healthcare improvement literature. BMJ Qual Saf. 2016;25(12):e7. doi:10.1136/bmjqs-2015-004480. PMID: 26385936.

Apply when reporting studies that describe healthcare quality, safety, or value improvement interventions in local or organizational contexts. SQUIRE 2.0 acknowledges that QI work is complex, context-dependent, and iterative. Items should be addressed as fully as their relevance allows; not all items apply to every study.

---

## Title and Abstract

### Item 1 -- Title

**Description:** Indicate that the manuscript concerns an initiative to improve healthcare (broadly defined to include the quality, safety, effectiveness, patient-centeredness, timeliness, efficiency, and equity of healthcare).

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- Report title naming the improvement initiative, care setting, and target outcome
- Title indicating the healthcare context (e.g., hospital, clinic, health system)
- Keywords including improvement-relevant terms (quality improvement, patient safety, implementation)
- Language distinguishing from a traditional clinical research study

### Item 2 -- Abstract

**Description:** Summarize the background and importance of the problem, the aim, setting, participant/patient population, study design and analytical methods, key findings, and conclusions.

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- Abstract including the specific problem and its measured baseline magnitude
- Aim and primary measure of improvement stated in abstract
- Setting and population described in abstract
- Key results with pre/post or time-series comparison reported

---

## Introduction

### Item 3 -- Problem description

**Description:** Nature and significance of the local problem. Describe what is known about the problem, why it is important to address, and how it affects patients, staff, or the organization.

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- Baseline performance data or measure of the problem's magnitude
- Patient harm, inefficiency, or inequity quantified at the local level
- Local context differentiated from the general literature
- Stakeholder identification (who is affected and how)

### Item 4 -- Available knowledge

**Description:** Summary of what is currently known about the problem, including relevant previous studies.

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- Literature review documenting prior QI or implementation studies addressing the same problem
- Evidence base for the proposed intervention (clinical guidelines, systematic reviews)
- Identification of successful implementations elsewhere and their key features
- Gaps between evidence and current local practice documented

### Item 5 -- Rationale

**Description:** Informal or formal frameworks, models, concepts, and/or theories used to explain the problem, any reasons or assumptions that were used to develop the intervention(s), and reasons why the intervention(s) was expected to work.

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- Theoretical framework cited (e.g., Model for Improvement, Lean, Theory of Constraints, Behavior Change Wheel)
- Driver diagram, logic model, or causal map linking intervention components to outcomes
- Assumptions about local factors that will support or hinder the intervention
- Mechanism of action described (how the intervention will change behavior or process)

### Item 6 -- Specific aims

**Description:** Purpose of the project and of this report.

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- Specific, measurable aim statement with target, timeline, and population
- SMART aim format (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound)
- Primary and secondary aims distinguished
- Alignment between aim statement and analytic approach

---

## Methods

### Item 7 -- Context

**Description:** Contextual elements considered important at the outset of introducing the intervention(s). Describe the setting, the local and system factors that were important for understanding the problem, and the people involved in delivering care.

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- Care setting described (hospital unit, ambulatory clinic, community setting)
- Local organizational factors documented (staffing, resources, culture, competing priorities)
- Patient population characteristics relevant to the improvement aim
- Historical context of prior improvement efforts at the site

### Item 8 -- Intervention(s)

**Description:** Description of the intervention(s) in sufficient detail that others could reproduce it. Describe what was done for whom, by whom, in what setting and when (the what, who, where, and when of the intervention).

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- Intervention components described with sufficient detail for replication
- Stakeholders involved in intervention delivery identified
- Timing and duration of the intervention specified
- Change package or bundle components documented
- Iterative refinements to the intervention over time described

### Item 9 -- Study of the intervention

**Description:** Approach used to assess the impact of the intervention(s). Describe the type of study design used (e.g., pre-post, interrupted time series, run chart, control chart).

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- Study design specified (pre-post, interrupted time series, stepped wedge, concurrent control)
- Unit of analysis identified (patient encounter, individual patient, provider, unit)
- Run charts or statistical process control charts generated
- Baseline and follow-up measurement periods defined

### Item 10 -- Measures

**Description:** Measures chosen for studying processes and outcomes of the intervention(s), including rationale for choosing them, their operational definitions, and how data were collected.

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- Outcome measures operationally defined (numerator, denominator, data source)
- Process measures defined alongside outcome measures
- Balancing measures identified (to detect unintended consequences)
- Measurement frequency and data collection method specified
- Reliability or validity of measures documented

### Item 11 -- Analysis

**Description:** Approach to assessing the impact of the intervention(s). Include the approach to analyzing quantitative data (e.g., statistical methods and software used) and qualitative data (e.g., content analysis, thematic analysis).

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- Statistical method specified and appropriate for study design (SPC, ITS regression, difference-in-differences)
- Run chart or SPC rules for special cause variation defined and applied in code
- Segmented regression or ITS model code for interrupted time series
- Qualitative analysis method described for mixed-methods components
- Software and package versions specified

### Item 12 -- Ethical considerations

**Description:** Ethical aspects of implementing and studying the improvement, including potential conflicts of interest, how they were managed, and the process for obtaining any required institutional permissions.

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- IRB or ethics board determination (QI exemption vs. full review) documented
- Informed consent process described or waiver documented
- Conflict of interest disclosures
- Potential dual-role conflicts (clinician-researcher) acknowledged and managed

---

## Results

### Item 13 -- Results

**Description:** Initial steps of the intervention and the context in which it was implemented; changes to the intervention during the project (with rationale); details of the process measures and outcome measures; and any harms to participants and unintended consequences.

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- Timeline of intervention implementation with PDSA cycles or phases documented
- Run charts or SPC charts for primary process and outcome measures over time
- Baseline and post-intervention descriptive statistics reported
- Any modifications to the intervention documented with rationale
- Balancing measures reported to detect unintended consequences
- Participant flow (patients or encounters included vs. excluded from measurement)

---

## Discussion

### Item 14 -- Summary

**Description:** Key findings, including relevance to the rationale and specific aims. Consider the overall implications with respect to the aim.

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- Summary of observed improvement magnitude relative to the stated aim
- Process measure findings linked to outcome measure findings
- Alignment between theoretical framework predictions and observed results
- Practical significance of improvement assessed alongside statistical signal

### Item 15 -- Interpretation

**Description:** Nature of the association between the intervention(s) and the outcomes; any observed mechanisms; unanticipated findings; sustainability; and transferability of findings.

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- Causal pathway between intervention components and outcomes discussed
- Rival explanations (secular trends, concurrent changes) addressed
- Unanticipated consequences identified and explained
- Sustainability of improvement over time assessed in time series data
- Transferability to other settings discussed with relevant contextual factors

### Item 16 -- Limitations

**Description:** Limits to the generalizability of the work. Include factors that may limit internal validity such as confounding, bias, or imprecision in the design, methods, measurement, or analysis.

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- Internal validity limitations (confounding, selection bias, measurement bias) discussed
- Absence of a concurrent control group acknowledged as limitation
- Hawthorne effect or regression to the mean addressed
- Local context features that may limit transferability identified

### Item 17 -- Conclusions

**Description:** Usefulness of the work; sustainability; potential for spread to other contexts; implications for practice and for further study in the field.

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- Actionable conclusions for similar settings
- Spread strategy for scaling the intervention
- Recommended adaptations for different contexts
- Implications for future QI research or evaluation

---

## Other Information

### Item 18 -- Funding

**Description:** Sources of funding that supported this work. Role, if any, of the funding organization in the design, implementation, interpretation, and reporting.

**What to look for in code/outputs:**
- Funding acknowledgment in report
- Grant numbers or organizational support documented
- Role of funder in QI project design or data access stated
- Conflict of interest disclosures for all authors
